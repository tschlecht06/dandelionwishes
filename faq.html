Common Questions

Starting therapy can feel like a big step. Here are answers to the questions families ask most.

About Play Therapy

What is play therapy?
Play therapy is a research-supported approach to counseling that uses play — toys, games, art, movement, and storytelling — as the primary way children communicate. Just as adults talk through problems, children use play to express what they can't yet put into words. The therapist creates a safe, non-directive environment where the child leads, and healing happens naturally through that play.
What ages do you work with?
I work with adolescents ages 3-12, teenagers and young adults.
What does play therapy look like?

Play is a child’s natural language. In play therapy, children use toys, creative materials, storytelling, movement, and imaginative play to express thoughts and feelings that may be difficult to put into words.

Through carefully selected activities and a supportive therapeutic relationship, children can work through worries, practice coping skills, and develop emotional understanding in a way that feels natural and engaging.

How involved are parents in therapy?

Parents are an important part of the therapeutic process. Depending on your child’s age and needs, parent check-ins are built into treatment to share progress, discuss strategies, and support growth outside of sessions.

At the same time, children also benefit from having a private space where they can build trust and express themselves freely.

Getting Started

How do I get started?

The first step is scheduling an initial consultation or intake session. This gives us a chance to discuss your concerns, ask questions, and determine whether services are a good fit for your family.

What happens in the first session?

The first session is an intake session with parents or guardians only. This gives us time to talk openly about your child’s history, current concerns, strengths, and the goals you have for therapy without your child needing to be present right away.

We will discuss important background information such as developmental history, family relationships, school experiences, and recent challenges or transitions. This session also gives you time to ask questions and learn more about how therapy may support your child.

How do I know if my child needs therapy?

Many children benefit from therapy when they are having difficulty managing emotions, behaviors, relationships, or changes in daily life. You may notice signs such as increased anxiety, frequent meltdowns, trouble separating, changes in sleep or appetite, school difficulties, withdrawal, irritability, grief, or behaviors that feel out of character for your child.

Sometimes children do not yet have the words to explain what they are feeling, so emotions may show up through behavior, play, or physical complaints. Therapy can provide a supportive space for children to express themselves, build coping skills, and strengthen emotional resilience.

Parents often reach out not because something is “wrong,” but because they want extra support helping their child through a challenging season, big transition, or ongoing concern.

How long does therapy typically last?

The length of therapy depends on each child’s goals, needs, and pace. Some children benefit from short-term support around a specific issue, while others benefit from longer-term work for deeper emotional growth.

Progress is reviewed regularly and treatment goals are adjusted together as needed. If we can schedule weekly sessions at least for the first 8-12 weeks, I tend to see progress more quickly. It is an investment, but means we can then assess and wean off slowly if appropriate after that.

How long are sessions?

Most therapy sessions are 45–55 minutes long. Parent consultation sessions may occasionally be scheduled differently depending on need.

Consistency is important, and many families begin with weekly sessions before adjusting frequency over time.

Do you work with teens?

Yes. Therapy for teens often includes a balance of conversation, creative strategies, emotional skill-building, and practical support tailored to their developmental needs.

Teens may seek support for anxiety, self-esteem, friendships, school stress, emotional overwhelm, grief, identity, or life transitions.

What if my child is nervous about therapy?

It is very common for children to feel unsure at first. Sessions are paced gently, and building comfort and trust is an important part of the process.

Children are never pressured to talk before they are ready. Often, connection develops naturally through play, conversation, and shared activities.

Do you offer parent coaching?

Yes. Parent coaching can help caregivers better understand behavior, strengthen connection, and develop practical strategies that support emotional growth at home.

Sometimes small changes in family patterns can make a meaningful difference for a child.

Is therapy confidential?

Confidentiality is an important part of therapy. Information shared is kept private except when disclosure is required by law, such as safety concerns involving harm to self, harm to others, or abuse/neglect.

For children and teens, confidentiality is balanced thoughtfully with parent involvement. I protect your child’s privacy to build trust while also keeping you informed about themes, progress, and areas of growth. Rather than sharing every detail, I focus on helping you understand what your child is working on and how you can support them effectively at home
